Facebook has been in limelight for not so good reasons for the past few weeks after the recent [Cambridge-Analytica news](http://www.bbc.com/news/technology-43461865) hit the media outlets. Facebook is being questioned by many government bodies across the globe regarding the data security, data privacy, and regulatory issues.

With over 2 billion registered users on Facebook, it is fair to say that Facebook has become an important and significant part of our society. As they say, "With great power comes great responsibility". But Facebook has failed to show its responsible side in the recent past.

Some of the Facebook's action that became public recently have shown how Facebook has transformed itself into a huge advertisement engine which only cares about its revenue growth over everything else.

Let's have a look at the facts that question Facebook's credibility as a trustworthy platform and why we might leave it as well.

## 1: Lack of data security
In the current debacle of Cambridge-Analytica, it is reported [Cambridge-Analytica had data of over 50 million Facebook users](https://www.theguardian.com/news/2018/mar/17/cambridge-analytica-facebook-influence-us-election) which was gathered by a professor at Cambridge University. It was collected by using an app on the Facebook platform. Even though only 270,000 people used this application, Facebook allowed the developer to collect information about the friends of the user also.

This data was then used by Cambridge-analytica to help political parties across the world to target and influence people. With detailed user data available, even the ad content was modified to manipulate users views on the candidates. With Christopher Wylie, a former employee of Cambridge-Analytica coming out and revealing the company's involvement across the world, we are yet to understand the full effect of this data leak.

If an application can collect this much amount of data with Facebook not even noticing or regulating the usage, then with millions of applications on Facebook, it is very evident that Facebook has very little idea of the overall data in the hands of Facebook application developers.

## 2. Collecting more than required user information
We all know that Facebook holds

* Personal information: Name, Email, Phone Number, Workplace, Age etc
* Interests: Movies, Music, Books, Sports, Celebrities etc
* Personal content & Activities: Friends, Family, Messages, Photos, Videos, Likes, Comments etc
* Session information & Ads: IP address, Device details, Session cookies, Ads liked, Ads blocked etc

The above list is already a huge set of personal information of a user, but Facebook collects more data over and above this.

Facebook collects your phone contacts list, [phone logs and messages](https://www.huffingtonpost.ca/2018/03/27/facebook-call-data-collection_a_23396393/) and the list applications that you installed in your phone. This is simply to say that Facebook knows who all you talk to and message every day and which apps you use. Facebook has been collecting this information since 2015 with most of the users not knowing about it.

Facebook claims that this information is shared with consent by the user. But with a simple permission asking to "Access contact list", it is very unclear for a user to understand what all data is being accessed.

Last week [Facebook admitted to collecting data of non-users](http://www.theweek.co.uk/cambridge-analytica/92862/facebook-admits-collecting-data-of-non-users). So even if you are not on Facebook or not using it at the time, Facebook collects data from the websites that you visit which use Facebook plugins.

## 3. Lack of control over fake news
Corporations and political parties using computational propaganda(Use of algorithms, automation, and human curation to purposefully distribute misleading information) to trolls creating fake content, social media networks are being exploited by people to spread fake news.

With a lot of people relying on social media as their primary source of information, these fake news tend to influence people's actions. The consequences can range from [invoking a communal violence](http://indianexpress.com/article/india/west-bengal-communal-violence-one-arrested-for-sharing-fake-image-and-inciting-passion-4742488/) to [influencing other country's elections](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/11/01/us/politics/russia-2016-election-facebook.html).

Amidst all of this, Facebook has been [very passive in creating some strict regulations](https://www.engadget.com/2018/01/19/facebooks-fake-war-on-fake-news/) to restrict these activities as they affect their ad revenue and user engagement. It took Facebook 2 years to admit that [Russian firms used Facebook Ads to influence 2016 US presidential election](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/facebook-says-it-sold-political-ads-to-russian-company-during-2016-election/2017/09/06/32f01fd2-931e-11e7-89fa-bb822a46da5b_story.html?utm_term=.8897469d8389).

## Conclusion:

> When a platform that serves over 2 billion people ignores or just de-prioritizes user privacy, data security and regulatory actions to control the activities, it poses a huge threat to the society.

In short, Facebook collects as much of data about you in every way possible, it chooses to not educate its users about any kind of breach unless it is forced by the external pressure, it refuses to accept how its platform can be used for wrong reasons and take strong action against it.

In this digitally connected world, it is important that people understand that what data these services collect and what do they do with it. It is not just Facebook which collects too much data about you, has data security issues or uses your data in an unethical way. Every major corporation does these things at some magnitude.

But with enough backing for the Facebook's case, it is safe to say that [deleting our Facebook accounts](https://fossbytes.com/how-to-delete-your-facebook-account-permanently/) makes more sense now than ever.
